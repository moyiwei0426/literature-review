## WRITING GUIDANCE — Follow These Patterns

These patterns are distilled from the review_learning corpus of high-quality pedestrian/traffic behavior reviews.

### Core rules:
- **No underscores** in technical terms. "gap_acceptance_threshold" → "gap acceptance threshold"; "e_hmi" → "eHMI"; "av" → "AV"
- **No placeholder text**. Never write "Section X synthesizes Y matrix rows." Write actual sentences with specific evidence anchors.
- **Cite by paper_id** or specific finding when making claims.
- **Open with substance**, not chronology or hype. No "Recent years have seen..." openings.
- **Close sections with synthesis or tension**, not summary recap.

### Per-section patterns:

**Introduction**: Stakes (quantified) → context narrowing → modeling challenge → literature fragmentation → scope → contribution roadmap.

**Methods/Taxonomy**: Announce number of paradigm families → per-family: definition + inputs + evidence + strengths + limitations → cross-family synthesis on ecological validity vs. interpretability tradeoff.

**Factors/Determinants**: Announce taxonomy → per-cluster: consensus first, then contradiction with explanation, then moderator identification → cross-cluster synthesis.

**Gap section**: Each gap entry must contain: (1) gap statement, (2) partial evidence anchor, (3) insufficiency reason, (4) consequence, (5) needed study or resolution. Prefer structured fields such as partial_evidence_paper_ids, partial_evidence_summary, why_insufficient, practical_consequence, research_need, and resolution_needed when available.

**Conclusion**: Field-level framing (NOT section-by-section recap) → 2-3 stable conclusions → 2-3 unresolved tensions with attribution → 2-3 research priorities directly tied to named gaps.

### Useful phrase anchors (copy-paste these constructions directly):
- "The evidence consistently identifies [factor] as [direction] influence on [outcome], with [specific threshold/effect] reported in [N] studies."
- "By contrast, [Study X] found [opposite/null finding], a divergence likely attributable to [methodological difference]."
- "When considered together, these findings suggest [mechanism], but only under conditions where [boundary condition]..."
- "Gap [N] concerns... Partial evidence: [Study X] examined [scope] and reported [finding], but this work is limited by [specific gap element]..."
- "This gap is particularly severe because [structural reason], and addressing it would require [specific research design]..."
- "The field is converging on a view in which pedestrian crossing decisions in AV environments are primarily calibrated to..."
- "Resolving this tension will require [specific design/data/longitudinal approach]..."
- "Priority should be given to [gap area], which carries direct implications for [practical outcome]..."

## Writing Principles

1. **No generic hype.** Do not open sections with "Recent years have seen..." or "In recent times...". Get into the topic directly with the research problem.
2. **Evidence-grounded.** Every substantive claim must be tied to a specific paper's claim_text, metric, or limitation from the matrix. Cite by paper_id where possible.
3. **Precise over comprehensive.** It is better to precisely characterize 3 papers than vaguely mention 10.
4. **Gap-aware.** Sections on Gaps must specifically reference which gap statements are being addressed and why the evidence supports them.
5. **Avoid placeholder text.** Never write "Section X synthesizes Y matrix rows." Write actual sentences.
6. **Use proper English.** Replace underscores with spaces in technical terms. "lane_formation_reproduction" → "lane formation reproduction". "gap_acceptance_prediction" → "gap acceptance prediction".

### Example: Introduction for a pedestrian crossing behavior review

**Outline:**
- section_id: sec-1
- title: Introduction and Research Context
- objective: Establish the review's motivation, scope, and why understanding pedestrian crossing behavior at signalized intersections matters for traffic safety and AV deployment.

**Good output:**
"Pedestrian fatalities at urban intersections represent a significant fraction of road traffic deaths globally. Among the contributing factors, signalized intersections present a particular tension: traffic signal regulations require pedestrians to wait even when gaps in vehicle traffic appear safe, yet empirical observation consistently shows that many pedestrians cross during red phases or initiate crossing before the walk interval begins. Understanding the behavioral mechanisms underlying these decisions has become increasingly urgent as automated vehicles enter urban road networks, requiring accurate models of pedestrian crossing intent to ensure safe human-machine interaction.

This review synthesizes findings from [N] studies examining pedestrian crossing behavior at signalized intersections, with a focus on three operational dimensions: the decision-making heuristics pedestrians employ when evaluating gap acceptability, the influence of signal timing and vehicular flow on waiting time tolerance, and the extent to which established behavioral models — originally developed for human-driven traffic — transfer to contexts involving autonomous vehicles. The reviewed evidence draws primarily from observational field studies, controlled experiments, and simulation-based investigations conducted across diverse geographic and infrastructural settings.

The motivation for a systematic comparison is threefold. First, existing models of pedestrian crossing behavior vary substantially in their theoretical foundations — from cognitive models grounded in collision risk perception to statistical models derived from revealed preference data — yet the literature lacks structured comparison across these approaches. Second, the rapid deployment of autonomous vehicles has introduced novel interaction scenarios, such as vehicle-to-pedestrian communication and yielding behavior, that existing reviews have not adequately addressed. Third, evaluation practices in the reviewed studies are heterogeneous, making cross-study comparison of model predictive performance difficult without a unified analytical framework."

### Example: Methods section for a pedestrian crossing behavior review

**Outline:**
- section_id: sec-2
- title: Methodological Approaches
- objective: Categorize and compare the modeling paradigms used to represent pedestrian crossing decisions.

**Good output:**
"The reviewed literature employs four distinct modeling paradigms, which can be organized by their representational basis: cognitive-psychological models, discrete choice models, cellular automaton and social force models, and data-driven machine learning approaches.

Cognitive-psychological models ground pedestrian crossing decisions in perceived collision risk, typically operationalizing the visual angle (θ) and its temporal derivative (θ̇) as primary determinants of the perceived time-to-collision. Studies in this tradition report that pedestrians are more likely to initiate crossing when the perceived collision threat exceeds a subjective threshold, though the estimated threshold values vary substantially across age groups and traffic environments.

Discrete choice models — predominantly binary logit and probit specifications — treat crossing initiation as a binary outcome determined by a vector of explanatory variables including waiting time, gap size, vehicle speed, and pedestrian age or gender. The evidence indicates that waiting time is the single strongest predictor of crossing violation propensity, with pedestrians exhibiting near-linear increases in violation probability as waiting time increases beyond approximately 30–40 seconds at typical urban signal timings.

Cellular automaton (CA) and social force (SF) models represent pedestrians as particles subject to repulsive interactions with other pedestrians, vehicles, and environmental boundaries. CA models discretize space and time into grid cells and update pedestrian positions according to transition rules, while SF models represent pedestrian motion as a continuous optimization of social repulsion and attractive forces toward a destination. Both approaches have been applied to evaluate signal timing policies, though the evidence suggests SF models capture emergent group behaviors — such as collective waiting at red lights — that CA models reproduce less faithfully.

Machine learning approaches, including random forests, support vector machines, and neural networks, have more recently been applied to predict pedestrian crossing intention from video or sensor data. These models achieve higher predictive accuracy on benchmark datasets than regression-based alternatives, but the evidence for their transport planning utility is limited: they do not yet support counterfactual policy analysis as readily as structural models do.

Across these four paradigms, a clear tension emerges between interpretive richness and predictive performance. Cognitive-psychological and discrete choice models provide interpretable parameters that can inform policy, while data-driven models excel at pattern recognition but offer less insight into causal mechanisms."

### Example: Gap section

**Outline:**
- section_id: sec-4
- title: Research Gaps and Opportunities
- objective: Present verified gaps and explain what specific evidence substantiates each.

**Good output:**
"The comparative analysis reveals three persistent gaps that current approaches have not adequately resolved.

Gap g1 concerns the limited cross-cultural validity of gap acceptance thresholds. The evidence substantiating specific critical gap values — typically in the range of 3–5 seconds for crossing from a stopped position — derives overwhelmingly from studies conducted in North American, European, and East Asian urban contexts. Whether these thresholds transfer to pedestrians in South Asian, African, or Latin American traffic environments, where traffic control norms and vehicle-pedestrian interaction patterns differ substantially, remains untested. This gap matters because infrastructure investment decisions in rapidly motorizing regions are being informed by models whose calibrations may not generalize.

Gap g2 concerns the absence of validated behavioral models for pedestrian interactions with autonomous vehicles. While the reviewed literature includes several studies examining AV-pedestrian interaction, these studies typically use a small number of AV prototypes in controlled settings and measure behavioral responses over short exposure periods. Whether pedestrians update their crossing heuristics as they accumulate experience with AVs — learning, for instance, that AVs consistently yield — remains an open question.

Gap g3 concerns the underdeveloped integration of smartphone distraction into crossing behavior models. The reviewed evidence consistently identifies mobile phone use as a significant predictor of delayed crossing initiation and increased violation frequency, yet the majority of studies treat smartphone distraction as a binary variable (distracted versus not distracted) rather than modeling the specific cognitive demands of different phone tasks. Without finer-grained measurement, the magnitude of the distraction effect may be either over- or under-estimated for policy purposes."
