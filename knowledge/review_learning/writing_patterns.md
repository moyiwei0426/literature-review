# Distilled Writing Patterns — Pedestrian / Traffic Behavior Reviews

## Core principle
Separate **evidence aggregation** from **argument construction**. Good reviews do not merely list findings; they frame a problem, partition the literature, compare approaches under explicit dimensions, and end each section with a synthesis or unresolved tension.

## Target patterns to learn

### 1. Opening strategy
- **Problem-first**, not chronology or hype.
- Sequence: quantified stakes → specific context narrowing → literature fragmentation → scope + contribution.
- Phrase anchors:
  - "A consistent picture has yet to emerge..."
  - "This heterogeneity in findings across contexts suggests..."
  - "The behavioral mechanisms underlying X have proven difficult to characterize because..."
  - "Whether ... remains an open question, in part because..."

### 2. Evidence-grounded claims
- Every substantive claim needs a specific anchor: paper ID, effect size, direction, or limitation.
- Never: "Studies show..." / "Research indicates..."
- Always: "[Study X] reported a [direction] effect of [magnitude] on [outcome]..." / "In contrast, [Study Y] found no such relationship..."
- Cross-paper synthesis: "The evidence across [N] studies converges on..." / "findings diverge primarily on [dimension], likely due to [moderator]..."

### 3. Contrastive transitions
- Use when comparing methods, findings, or populations:
  - "By contrast," — introduce an opposing or different finding
  - "In practice," — bridge theory/idealization with real-world complexity
  - "Taken together, these findings suggest..." — synthesize convergent evidence
  - "A different pattern emerges when..." — pivot after presenting a body of evidence
  - "This tension remains unresolved because..." — flag unresolved conflict

### 4. Section-ending strategies
Each section should close with **one of**:
- **Synthesis statement**: integrates findings from this section into a coherent picture
- **Unresolved contradiction**: highlights where evidence conflicts without forced resolution
- **Methodological bottleneck**: notes where the evidence base is methodologically limited
- **Coverage gap**: identifies a sub-topic, population, or context that has been underexplored
- **Transition**: sets up the next section's argument

Anti-pattern for section endings: summary recap ("This section reviewed X, Y, Z...")

### 5. Taxonomy/section organization
- Announce the organizing principle explicitly: "We partition the evidence into three clusters: ..."
- Never organize by paper-by-paper sequence.
- Each cluster gets: definition → evidence → consensus → contradiction → moderator note.

### 6. Gap-writing pattern
Each gap entry must contain:
1. **Gap statement** (what specifically is unknown or insufficient)
2. **Partial evidence** (what has been done, named specifically)
3. **Insufficiency reason** (why existing evidence doesn't resolve it)
4. **Consequence** (why this matters for the field or for practice)

Phrase anchors:
- "Gap [N] concerns... The evidence substantiating this claim derives from [Study X], which found..., yet this work is limited by..."
- "A persistent gap concerns..., for which the existing evidence base is insufficient because most studies..."
- "This gap is particularly severe because [structural reason], and addressing it would require..."

### 7. Future-work / conclusion anchors
- Must tie directly to **named gaps**, not generic "more research needed."
- Phrase anchors:
  - "Resolving this tension will require [specific design / data / longitudinal approach]..."
  - "Priority should be given to [gap area], which carries direct implications for [practical outcome]..."
  - "The highest-priority empirical need is for [specific study type] that..."

## Anti-patterns to avoid
1. "Recent years have seen..." / "In recent times..." — chronological framing, no substance
2. "This paper/review discusses..." — author-centric, no evidence
3. Flat one-sentence-per-paper listing — no synthesis
4. "Studies show X" without citing which, how many, or with what effect size
5. Section endings that are summaries rather than synthesis or tension
6. Generic future work: "more research is needed" without gap anchoring
7. Claims without distinguishing: descriptive summary vs. cross-paper comparison vs. evaluative synthesis
8. Mixing up correlation and causation language in effect descriptions

## Phrase-level building blocks

### Evidence introduction
- "The evidence for [claim] is broadly consistent, though the magnitude varies substantially with [moderator]..."
- "[Study X] reported that [finding], with an effect size of [ES] on [outcome]..."
- "By contrast, [Study Y] found [opposite/nul finding], a divergence likely attributable to [methodological difference]..."
- "When considered together, these findings suggest [mechanism], but only under conditions where [boundary condition]..."

### Contrasting findings
- "This is in contrast to [Study X], yet similar to [Study Y]..."
- "One study examining [context] also found [finding]..."
- "A notable moderator across studies is [variable], which appears to condition the direction of the effect..."
- "The key unresolved question is whether [mechanism], because [specific evidence gap]..."

### Synthesizing
- "The evidence across [N] studies converges on a field-level picture in which..."
- "Three stable conclusions emerge from this review: first,... second,... third..."
- "Two persistent tensions run through this literature: first..., and second..."
- "The reviewed literature is characterized by substantial heterogeneity in [dimension], making direct comparison difficult without..."

### Limitation expressions
- "This approach cannot distinguish whether... because..."
- "The generalizability of this finding is limited by [specific constraint]..."
- "Ecological validity concerns have been raised regarding [method], given that..."
- "Transfer to [context] remains largely untested because..."

### Methodological assessment
- "The dominant approach in this literature has been [method], which offers [strength] but is limited by..."
- "Only a small subset of studies additionally validated their instruments against..."
- "A critical gap in the validation literature is the absence of studies comparing [A] against [B]..."
- "The absence of a unified benchmark dataset spanning [dimensions] makes cross-study comparison problematic."

## Separating: Description vs. Comparison vs. Evaluation
- **Descriptive summary**: "Study X used [method] to examine [question] and found [result]."
- **Cross-paper comparison**: "Studies using [method A] consistently find [direction], whereas [method B] studies report [direction] or null effects." — requires multiple studies
- **Evaluative synthesis**: "The weight of evidence favors [conclusion] over [alternative], on the basis of [criteria: effect consistency, sample size, methodological rigor]." — requires evaluative framework
